Eva C. Sonnenschein, PhD

Biofilms are the cities of the microbial world. In rapidly changing environments like aquatic systems, surface association allows localization in a micro-environment providing beneficial nutrient conditions and protection. The inhabitants of these cities communicate via small molecules. A broad range of these diverse bioactive compounds has been discovered including now clinically relevant antibiotics; however, their natural role in “microbe chat” remains unknown. In aquatic systems, phytoplankton organisms provide a major surface for heterotrophic bacteria. These tiny microalgae are responsible for 50% of the global carbon fixation and thus, the interaction of phytoplankton and bacteria build the basis for energy flux and nutrient cycling in the oceans.

My research aims to understand the molecular details of this interaction. I am fascinated by microbes, how they communicate and interact in beneficial and detrimental ways on a microscopic scale. I study these interaction pathways on the gene, RNA, protein and metabolite level with the overall goal to predict how chemical communication controls and is controlled by the habitat and globally speaking, how ecosystems adapt to environmental changes.
